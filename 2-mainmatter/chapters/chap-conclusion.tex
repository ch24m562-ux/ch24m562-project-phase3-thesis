\chapter{Discussion and Conclusion}
\label{chap:conclusion}
\setlength{\emergencystretch}{1.5em}

Chapter~\ref{chap:results} reported what the experimental evidence
shows. This chapter discusses what it means: which design choices the
evidence actually supports, why the scenario-dependent and null results
occurred rather than the uniformly positive result originally
hypothesised, what this thesis contributes, its limitations, and the
research directions that follow.

% ============================================================
\section{Summary of the Work}
\label{sec:conclusion_summary}
% ============================================================

This thesis investigated whether a unified reinforcement learning
agent, jointly optimising energy dispatch and diesel inventory
ordering, improves the operational reliability of off-grid and
weak-grid telecom base stations relative to decoupled classical and
optimisation-based approaches. The gap motivating this work is
practical: prior RL-based energy management for remote base stations
routinely models battery dispatch and generator scheduling but omits
the upstream replenishment decision --- when and how much diesel to
order --- despite this being a primary driver of service interruption
in fuel-constrained deployments.

The problem was framed as a constrained Markov decision process in
which a single policy (RLInv) controls generator commitment and diesel
order quantity jointly, with battery dispatch handled by a fixed
priority rule and an 11-dimensional observation vector including
explicit inventory and pipeline state (Chapter~\ref{chap:problem}).
RLInv was evaluated against eight comparator policies spanning
rule-based, reinforcement learning, and optimisation-based approaches ---
including, notably, a receding-horizon MPC baseline and a perfect-information
Oracle-MPC upper bound, added specifically to distinguish whether any
observed advantage reflects better \emph{ordering} or merely better
\emph{dispatch} (Chapter~\ref{chap:methodology}). The final campaign
evaluated all nine core policies across 10 ITU/Zindi sites, 10
independent training seeds, and four delivery-logistics scenarios of
increasing severity (Normal, Delayed, Monsoon, Extreme), using paired
seed-level statistical comparisons throughout
(Chapter~\ref{chap:experimental-execution}).

\section{Key Results and Discussion}
\label{sec:conclusion_results}
% ============================================================

\subsection{What the primary comparison shows}

Across the evaluated scenarios, RLInv consistently ranks among the
best-performing policies and achieves the lowest mean EENS in three of
the four representative logistics scenarios (Normal, Delayed, Extreme),
remaining competitive with the best classical baseline under Monsoon.
Critically, this advantage is not explained by superior
dispatch alone: a rolling-horizon MPC baseline with a classical
ordering rule performs no better than the simplest rule-based ordering
policy, and the gap to Oracle-MPC --- which has access to \emph{perfect}
future information for both forecasting and dispatch --- does not
shrink under increasing logistics stress and is in fact largest under
Monsoon. Taken together, this indicates that RLInv's advantage derives
substantially from the ordering decision, not from a forecasting or
dispatch capability that a sufficiently well-informed classical
controller could match. The advantage is also not achieved through
disproportionate fuel use: RLInv consumes only 3.6\% more diesel than
the best classical baseline (205.7 vs.\ 198.5\,kWh), consistent with a
genuine operational trade-off rather than an inflated comparison.

Read together, the Oracle-MPC comparison and the mechanism results
that follow point to a single underlying message. RLInv's contribution
is not that reinforcement learning is universally better than classical
optimisation --- Oracle-MPC, MPC, and the classical $(s,S)$ rule remain
strong references throughout. It is that joint optimisation of
replenishment and dispatch becomes valuable specifically under
uncertainty that classical rule-based coordination cannot easily
represent, and that this value is conditional on the logistics regime
rather than unconditional.

\subsection{Why the mechanism results are scenario-dependent, not uniform}

The three pre-registered hypotheses were tested through paired,
seed-level ablations rather than a single aggregate comparison, and the
honest answer in each case is more nuanced than a simple confirmed/rejected
verdict.

\textbf{Joint replenishment and dispatch learning (H1).} The cleaner
test (RLInv vs.\ A6, dispatch architecture held constant) shows a
significant RLInv advantage under Normal and Extreme logistics, no
difference under Delayed, and a result that nominally favours the
classical-ordering comparator under Monsoon. This is not the uniform
"joint learning always wins" result originally anticipated. Contrary to
the original hypothesis, the paired Phase 3 analysis shows that the
benefit of joint replenishment learning is scenario-dependent rather
than universal; this finding illustrates the value of large-scale
replication, which refined the original interpretation obtained from
the earlier pilot study. The most
defensible interpretation is that joint optimisation helps specifically
when the logistics regime is either mild enough that anticipatory
ordering has room to matter, or severe enough that classical
heuristics under-react; at intermediate stress (Monsoon), a
well-calibrated classical rule is difficult to improve on.

\textbf{Action masking (H2).} With the ordering mechanism held fixed
(A6 vs.\ TrackB, though these also differ in observation design ---
Chapter~\ref{chap:methodology}), masking is associated with a large,
highly significant effect,
but only under Monsoon and Extreme --- conditions the masked and
unmasked dispatch policies were never trained under. This is best
understood as an out-of-distribution robustness effect: masking
constrains exploration to the feasible action subspace during
training, producing a dispatch policy that generalises more reliably
to logistics conditions harsher than anything seen in training. Because
A6 and TrackB also differ in observation dimensionality, this effect
should not be attributed to masking alone; the cleaner masking
comparison, RLInv vs.\ A7 (which share the same observation space),
found no significant effect.
Separately, when ordering is already jointly learned (RLInv vs.\ A7),
masking has no measurable effect on mean EENS at any scenario ---
masking's benefit is conditional on externally managed replenishment,
not a universal property of the learning algorithm, and is not by
itself the explanation for RLInv's full advantage over TrackB.

\textbf{Inventory observability (H3).} RLInv achieves numerically lower
EENS than an ablation with inventory and ordering-history observations
removed, in every scenario, but the difference never reaches
significance (closest at Extreme, $p=0.058$). This is reported as a
genuine finding, not a shortfall: it suggests the agent can infer much
of the required information indirectly from other observable state
(battery state-of-charge, grid availability), so explicit inventory
information plays a secondary role relative to the joint control
architecture itself.

\subsection{Behaviour, robustness, and the exploratory extension}

Behavioural analysis shows RLInv's ordering frequency decreasing
monotonically from 2.16 orders per episode under Normal logistics to
1.34 under Extreme, consistent with the policy reducing new orders as
delivery completion within the evaluation horizon becomes increasingly
unlikely, while stockout events remain at zero even under Extreme
stress --- unmet demand arises from graceful load-shedding, not
uncontrolled fuel depletion. A continuous sweep across mean delivery
lead time shows RLInv's advantage over the best classical baseline
peaking at 29.5\,kWh around 72\,h and narrowing to 8.6\,kWh by 336\,h;
this narrowing reflects a physical constraint common to every policy
(tank capacity bounds how much can be pre-ordered regardless of
strategy), not RLInv ceasing to function.

The primary comparison is robust to reward-coefficient choice (EENS
varies by only 3.32\,kWh across a 20-fold range of the unmet-load
penalty), to tank capacity (RLInv's advantage vanishes only once the
physical buffer, at 336\,h of capacity, is large enough to absorb
delivery uncertainty without proactive ordering, confirming the
mechanism rather than undermining it), and to the parametric form
assumed for delivery delay (policy rankings are preserved under
lognormal and Weibull alternatives, with RLInv less sensitive to this
modelling choice than the classical baseline). One genuine limitation
was found under stochastic (rather than deterministic) grid outages: a
policy trained under this harder condition improves under Normal
logistics but degrades under Delayed, Monsoon, and Extreme, most
severely at Monsoon (+43.4\%) --- a real trade-off between adapting to
grid uncertainty and conserving diesel under prolonged delivery
disruption, reported as an identified limitation rather than resolved.

An exploratory extension (RLInv-DA) investigated whether explicit
delay-history tracking, motivated by Phase-I reviewer feedback,
improves on RLInv further. Despite a verified, working mechanism (a
correctly updating delay estimate under a persistent two-regime
supplier model), the extension produced no consistent EENS improvement
across the full evaluation grid. This null result is itself
informative: RLInv's existing state representation already captures
most of the information such delay estimation would provide, so the
added complexity is not justified at the scale evaluated.

\section{Novel Contributions}
\label{sec:conclusion_contributions}
% ============================================================

This thesis makes the following contributions to RL-based energy
management of remote telecom infrastructure:

\begin{enumerate}

  \item \textbf{A CMDP formulation with explicit, jointly controlled
  inventory and dispatch decisions.} The inclusion of diesel inventory
  and in-transit pipeline state alongside battery state-of-charge and
  grid availability enables anticipatory ordering that reacts to
  lead-time uncertainty before a stockout occurs, evaluated against
  eight comparator architectures rather than assumed superior.

  \item \textbf{A controlled empirical test of the coupling hypothesis
  against both RL and optimisation-based decomposition.} Rather than
  comparing only against other reinforcement-learning baselines, this
  work includes a receding-horizon MPC baseline and a perfect-information
  Oracle-MPC reference specifically to separate the contribution of
  ordering from the contribution of dispatch quality --- a comparison
  not present in the RL-energy-management literature reviewed in
  Chapter~\ref{chap:literature}. The result is a genuinely
  scenario-dependent finding, reported honestly rather than as a single
  uniform verdict.

  \item \textbf{Evidence that action masking's primary value is
  out-of-distribution robustness, not deployment-time constraint
  enforcement.} The paired ablation isolating masking from ordering
  mechanism shows a large effect specifically under logistics
  conditions harsher than the training distribution, clarifying what
  masking actually contributes in this setting.

  \item \textbf{A systematically verified, reproducible evaluation
  framework.} All primary results, ablations, and robustness checks
  (reward weighting, discount factor, tank capacity, distributional
  form, stochastic grid outages) are computed from a single canonical
  dataset (10 sites $\times$ 10 seeds $\times$ 4 scenarios, paired
  statistical methodology throughout), with every figure and
  statistic independently re-derived from source during this work
  rather than assumed correct.

  \item \textbf{An honestly reported exploratory extension.} The
  delay-aware extension (RLInv-DA) is reported as a verified-but-null
  result rather than omitted, on the basis that a well-implemented
  negative result is more scientifically useful than an unreported one.

\end{enumerate}

\section{Critique and Limitations}
\label{sec:conclusion_limitations}
% ============================================================

The following limitations define the scope within which these results
should be interpreted; each is a precise statement of scope rather than
an apology.

\begin{description}

  \item[Synthetic delivery-pipeline state.] The in-transit diesel
  quantity is the only state component not grounded directly in the
  ITU/Zindi dataset, since the dataset does not record delivery events.
  Its geometric (and, as a robustness check, lognormal and Weibull)
  parameterisation is a modelling choice, not an empirical distribution
  fitted to real vendor data.

  \item[Discrete, tank-relative order quantities.] Order quantities are
  restricted to three levels expressed as a percentage of tank capacity
  (0\%, 30\%, 60\%), rather than continuous or site-specific fixed
  litre amounts. This simplifies the action space but means the
  precise quantity granularity available to a real operator is not
  represented.

  \item[Single-tower scope.] Each site is evaluated as an independent
  control problem. Multi-tower coordination --- for example, several
  towers competing for delivery slots from a shared supplier during a
  regional disruption --- is outside the scope of the CMDP formulated
  here.

  \item[Stochastic grid outage trade-off unresolved.] Training under a
  stochastic rather than deterministic grid-outage model improves
  Normal-scenario performance but degrades performance under logistics
  stress; this trade-off is documented (Chapter~\ref{chap:results}) but
  not resolved within this thesis.

  \item[RLInv-DA evaluated on a narrower grid.] The delay-aware
  extension's evaluation uses three sites and three training seeds
  rather than the full ten-site, ten-seed grid used elsewhere, since it
  was scoped as an exploratory check rather than a primary result; its
  null finding should be read with this narrower scope in mind.

  \item[No real vendor delivery data.] Every distributional-robustness
  check (geometric, lognormal, Weibull) is a comparison between
  parametric assumptions, not a validation against observed delivery
  logs, which do not exist in the available dataset.

\end{description}

\section{Future Work}
\label{sec:conclusion_future}
% ============================================================

Several of the directions identified during the original project
proposal --- multi-scenario evaluation, an expanded seed count,
optimisation-based baselines, and cross-site robustness checks --- have
since been completed and are reported in
Chapter~\ref{chap:results} rather than remaining open. The four
directions below are ordered by how directly they continue this
thesis's own findings, from richer logistics modelling through to
deployment generalisation; two smaller extensions follow at the end.

\textbf{1.\ Richer logistics and supplier-behaviour modelling.} Two
results in this thesis point the same way. First, RLInv-DA showed that
a simple online estimator (EWMA) of realised delivery delay does not
improve replenishment at the evaluation scale tested. Following the
same progression EWMA itself follows in inventory-control practice, a
Kalman-filter or Bayesian estimator of supplier state would be the
natural next step before considering a learned forecasting model.
Second, training under stochastic grid outages improves Normal-scenario
performance at the cost of degraded performance under logistics stress;
a natural extension is to train jointly under stochastic grid
\emph{and} varied logistics scenarios simultaneously, testing whether
this trade-off is an artefact of modelling only one source of
uncertainty at a time. Both directions aim at the same underlying gap:
richer, more realistic supplier and infrastructure behaviour than the
geometric and two-state abstractions used here.

\textbf{2.\ Fleet-level multi-agent reinforcement learning.} Realistic
deployments often involve several towers sharing a common diesel
supplier and competing for delivery capacity during regional
disruptions. The single-tower CMDP formulated here cannot capture this
coordination problem; a multi-agent extension in which each tower is an
independent agent but lead times are coupled through shared logistics
capacity is the most direct route to operationally realistic deployment
at scale, and the natural fleet-level counterpart to the single-tower
joint-optimisation result established here.

\textbf{3.\ Validation against empirical delivery datasets.} Every
distributional-robustness check in this thesis (geometric, lognormal,
Weibull) compares parametric modelling assumptions against one another,
not against observed delivery logs, since none exist in the available
dataset. Partnering with a telecom operator or fuel logistics provider
to obtain real delivery-time records would allow the lead-time model
itself to be validated empirically, rather than only stress-tested
across plausible parametric forms.

\textbf{4.\ Transfer learning and cross-region deployment.} The
cross-site evaluation in this thesis trains and evaluates a separate
policy per site; it is a per-site robustness check, not a test of
whether a policy trained on a subset of sites transfers to unseen ones
or to a different deployment region. Training on a subset of sites and
evaluating on held-out sites, or on sites from a different geographic
region with different solar, load, and outage characteristics, would
test this directly and is the natural precursor to any claim of
practical, low-cost deployment across a large or geographically diverse
fleet.

Two smaller extensions follow directly from the objective function and
state representation already defined in this thesis, without requiring
new experimental infrastructure. \textbf{Carbon and cost multi-objective
reporting}: the objective function (Chapter~\ref{chap:problem}) includes
a carbon-cost term that the evaluation reports only implicitly through
diesel consumption; reporting EENS--CO$_2$ Pareto frontiers would test
whether the carbon penalty can reduce emissions without increasing
service interruptions. \textbf{Solar forecast integration}: RLInv
currently observes only realised PV output; augmenting the observation
with a short-horizon solar forecast, available in practice from
numerical weather prediction services, would let the agent anticipate
low-PV periods rather than reacting to them, a natural complement to
the delay-aware extension already explored on the ordering side.

\section{Closing Remarks}
\label{sec:conclusion_closing}
% ============================================================

This thesis set out to answer a specific question: does making the
diesel inventory pipeline visible to a reinforcement-learning
controller, and letting it decide when and how much to order,
measurably improve service reliability at fuel-constrained telecom
base stations, relative to both classical decomposition and
optimisation-based dispatch? The answer that emerges from a fully
verified, paired-statistics evaluation across ten sites, ten seeds, and
four logistics regimes is genuinely conditional: yes, under normal and
severe logistics stress, and not distinguishably under moderate
(Monsoon-scenario) stress, where a well-calibrated classical ordering
rule is difficult to improve on.

This conditionality is not a weaker result than a uniform "RL wins
everywhere" finding would have been --- it is a more useful one. A
practitioner deciding how to deploy energy-management software at a
remote site now has evidence about \emph{when} joint optimisation is
worth its added complexity, rather than a claim that it always is. The
decision to test three pre-registered hypotheses rather than assert
that reinforcement learning is unconditionally superior allowed the
scenario-dependent and null results (H1's Monsoon exception, H2's
conditional benefit, H3's non-significance, RLInv-DA's null finding) to
be reported as genuine evidence rather than suppressed in favour of a
cleaner narrative.

Energy security at remote telecom towers is not a niche engineering
problem. In many low- and middle-income countries, base station uptime
determines access to emergency communication, mobile banking, and
public health services, and diesel logistics account for a substantial
share of operating cost in weak-grid deployments. A controller that is
reliable under routine conditions and whose limits under logistics
stress are precisely characterised, rather than assumed away, is a
more trustworthy basis for real deployment decisions than one whose
advantages are reported without qualification.
