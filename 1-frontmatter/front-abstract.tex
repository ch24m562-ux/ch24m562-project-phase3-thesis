Reliable and low-carbon energy management for hybrid telecom tower sites
remains a critical challenge in regions with weak or intermittent electricity
supply. Conventional rule-based controllers and offline optimisation
approaches rely on fixed thresholds and deterministic assumptions,
making them unable to adapt to stochastic solar generation, grid outages, and
fuel logistics uncertainty.

This thesis studies the operational control of hybrid
solar--battery--diesel telecom tower systems under uncertainty in renewable
generation, grid availability, and diesel delivery. The problem is formulated
as a Constrained Markov Decision Process (CMDP) that explicitly models
battery operating constraints and diesel inventory dynamics, including diesel replenishment ordering and stochastic delivery lead times.

A data-grounded simulation environment is developed using the ITU~2024
Smart Energy Scheduling dataset, comprising operational traces from ten
telecom base station sites at hourly resolution. Reinforcement learning
policies are trained with hard action masking that prevents infeasible control actions at each decision step.

The proposed RLInv framework is evaluated
against eight comparator policies spanning rule-based,
reinforcement learning, and optimisation-based approaches, including a
receding-horizon Model Predictive Control (MPC) baseline and an
optimistic perfect-information Oracle-MPC reference. The evaluation
covers ten sites, ten independent training seeds, and four
delivery-logistics scenarios of increasing severity, with paired
seed-level statistical comparisons performed throughout.

Across the four logistics scenarios, RLInv achieves the lowest mean
Expected Energy Not Served (EENS) under Normal and Extreme logistics,
and is not statistically distinguishable from the closest comparator
under Delayed or Monsoon conditions. Overall,
these results indicate that RLInv's performance cannot be attributed to
superior dispatch or forecasting alone. The gap to Oracle-MPC, which
assumes perfect future information, is largest under Monsoon conditions
but narrows again under Extreme logistics, where physical fuel
availability increasingly constrains all policies.

Isolating the contribution of jointly learned replenishment through a
controlled ablation shows that its benefit is scenario-dependent rather
than universal. It is statistically significant under Normal and Extreme
logistics stress but is not distinguishable from a classical ordering
rule under Delayed or moderate (Monsoon)
logistics stress. Action masking is shown to improve dispatch-policy robustness under
logistics conditions that are harsher than the training distribution,
rather than acting as the primary safety mechanism during deployment.

Together, these findings indicate that jointly learning diesel
replenishment and energy dispatch enables reinforcement learning to
exploit sequential inventory--logistics interactions that are difficult
to capture through fixed rule-based coordination under uncertain fuel
delivery.

\end{doublespacing}
